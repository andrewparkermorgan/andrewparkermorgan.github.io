Andrew P Morgan, MD PhD

I am a physician-scientist trainee in internal medicine and pediatrics at Duke University Hospital. I am broadly interested in how evolutionary history shapes the distribution of risk alleles in contemporary populations, particularly on the sex chromosomes.

Research interests

The focus of my thesis was the evolution of the genome of the house mouse (Mus musculus). In particular I focused on the role of large duplications: these loci are highly variable beween species and contribute disproportionately to genetic variation within populations. I am interested in the population-genetic forces — recombination, mutation, selection and drift — that create and maintain this diversity.

I was supervised by Fernando Pardo-Manuel de Villena, whose lab anchors the UNC Systems Genetics research group.

Besides evolutionary questions, I also worked on the quantiative genetics of complex traits using the Collaborative Cross and Diversity Outbred mouse populations.

I am currently a resident in internal medicine and pediatrics at Duke University Hospital.
